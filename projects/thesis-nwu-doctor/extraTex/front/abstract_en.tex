\nwuFrontHeading{ABSTRACT}

The Qinling Mountains form a major geographic boundary and ecological security barrier in China. Complex terrain, climate gradients, and vegetation types jointly support rich biodiversity, while long-term monitoring and conservation planning require data from field transects, camera traps, remote sensing images, phenological records, and literature sources. These data usually differ in spatial scale, temporal continuity, taxonomic standard, and uncertainty level. This public example builds a doctoral dissertation scaffold around biodiversity data fusion, variable organization, conservation zoning, and management interpretation, so that the Northwest University doctoral template can demonstrate the required dissertation structure.

The example first organizes heterogeneous ecological observations through a quality-control workflow and maps species records, habitat factors, disturbance indicators, and management information into a reproducible data layer. It then constructs a cross-scale feature fusion framework to align field observations with gridded environmental variables. A conservation-priority workflow is further introduced to combine species richness, habitat connectivity, disturbance pressure, and uncertainty indicators into interpretable zoning results. Finally, the example explains how chapters, figures, tables, equations, references, doctoral achievements, acknowledgements, and author biography are rendered by the unified `bensz-thesis` pipeline. All names, data, and narratives are public demonstration materials.

At the typesetting level, this template follows the 2019 Northwest University graduate thesis specification and the public Word template from the Graduate School. It covers the Chinese cover, English title page, intellectual property statement, originality statement, Chinese and English abstracts, table of contents, chapters, conclusion, references, appendix, research achievements during doctoral study, acknowledgements, and author biography.
